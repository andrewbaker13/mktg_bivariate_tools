How to Choose a Tool

Start with the kind of outcome you are measuring and how your data were collected:

  • Two numeric metrics on the same people → association question → Pearson Correlation.
  • One numeric metric for two independent groups → mean comparison → Welch's t-test.
  • One numeric metric measured twice on the same people (before/after, A vs. B) → Paired t-test.
  • One numeric metric for three or more groups → One-Way ANOVA.
  • Binary outcome (convert vs. not, click vs. no click) for two independent groups → A/B Proportion tool.
  • Binary outcome measured twice on the same people (pre/post, control vs. treatment on the same accounts) → McNemar test.
  • Two categorical variables (e.g., device type by response, segment by offer) → Chi-square test of association.
  • One numeric outcome with multiple predictors → Multiple Linear Regression.
  • Binary outcome with multiple predictors → Logistic Regression.

Correlation & Regression

Pearson Correlation

Explores whether two continuous marketing metrics move together (for example, ad spend vs. revenue or time on site vs. conversion rate).

Data: pairs of numeric values for each case.

Bivariate Linear Regression

Fits a straight-line relationship between one predictor and one outcome, either for a continuous predictor (for example, weekly ad spend vs. revenue) or a binary group indicator.

Data: summary stats (n, means, SDs, r) or raw paired observations.

Multiple Linear Regression

Models a numeric outcome using multiple predictors (continuous and categorical) with diagnostics and effect plots (for example, customer value vs. recency, frequency, and segment).

Data: raw rows with one numeric outcome and several predictors.

Logistic Regression

Models a binary outcome using multiple predictors to estimate odds ratios and predicted probabilities (for example, conversion vs. not convert based on traffic source, offer, and device).

Data: raw rows with one binary outcome and several predictors.

Unsupervised & Advanced Models

K-Means Clustering

Segments records into a specified number of clusters based on numeric predictors, assigning each case to the nearest cluster center.

Data: raw rows with two or more numeric predictors for clustering.

Multinomial Logistic Regression

Models a categorical outcome with 3+ levels using multiple predictors, framed as a choice model.

Data: raw rows with one categorical outcome and multiple predictors.

Means & Proportions

Welch's t-test (Independent Means)

Compares average performance between two independent groups when variances and sample sizes may differ (for example, Control vs. New Subject Line).

Data: group means/SDs and sample sizes, or raw outcomes for each group.

Paired Sample t-test

Evaluates before/after or A/B measurements on the same people or accounts (for example, pre-course vs. post-course scores).

Data: paired numeric observations or paired summary statistics.

One-Way ANOVA

Tests whether three or more groups differ on a numeric outcome (for example, four landing page designs vs. revenue per visitor).

Data: group means/SDs and sample sizes, or long-format raw data.

A/B Proportion Tool

Compares conversion rates or other binary outcomes between two independent variants (for example, Newsletter CTA A vs. CTA B).

Data: conversions and sample sizes, or raw 0/1 outcomes by group.

Categorical & Matched-Pair Tests

Chi-square Test of Association

Checks whether two categorical variables are related (for example, device type by signup status or segment by offer selection).

Data: contingency table counts or raw categorical outcomes.

McNemar Test

Focuses on "switchers" in paired yes/no data, such as matched pre/post responses (for example, same customers under two creative treatments).

Data: 2 × 2 matched table or raw paired outcomes.

Planning & Sample Size

Sample Size Planner (Single outcome)

Plans how many observations you need to estimate or detect a change in a single mean or single proportion (for example, overall conversion rate or average order value).

Sample Size Planner (A/B tests)

Plans total sample size for A/B tests comparing two means or two proportions, given the lift that would be worth acting on, your confidence level, and desired power.

Sample Size Planner (Correlation / Regression)

Plans how many paired observations you need to detect a non-zero correlation or simple regression slope between two metrics (for example, media spend vs. revenue or engagement vs. satisfaction).

Sample Size Planner (Multi‑arm A/B)

Plans the per‑arm and total sample size for tests with a control and multiple variants (A/B/C/…), for either proportions or means, under goals focused on minimum lift vs. control and/or any meaningful difference across arms.

Sampling & Design

Sampling Designs Visualizer

Shows how simple random, stratified, cluster, systematic, and convenience sampling select individuals from the same population, and how each design affects the composition and variability of sample estimates.

Selection Probability Lab

Explores the probability that specific items are selected from a finite population under sampling with or without replacement, showing exact hypergeometric/binomial math, live equations, and simulated draws.

Tips for Students

  • Always sketch your research question in plain language before choosing a test.
  • Match the tool to the data structure (numeric vs. categorical, independent vs. paired).
  • Use the built-in narratives as a starting point, then rewrite them in your own words for assignments.
  • Experiment with the sample scenarios and then upload your own data once you are comfortable.

These apps are designed as teaching tools: they show equations, diagnostics, and APA-style reporting so you can see how the math connects to marketing decisions.
